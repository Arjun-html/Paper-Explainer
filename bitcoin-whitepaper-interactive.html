§ 01 · The problem

Introduction

“Commerce on the Internet has come to rely almost exclusively on financial institutions serving as trusted third parties to process electronic payments.”

What it's actually saying: in 2008, every online payment ran through an intermediary — a bank, a card network, PayPal. That works, but the intermediary is a load-bearing wall. Because banks can and do reverse payments (chargebacks, fraud disputes), no online payment is ever truly final. Merchants price that risk in, demand more personal information than they should need, and small casual payments become uneconomical because every transaction carries mediation overhead.

Physical cash doesn't have this problem — handing someone a bill is final and requires no referee. The paper's goal is electronic cash: a payment system with cash-like finality, where the thing that prevents cheating is not a trusted company but cryptographic proof plus economics.

The one-sentence preview of the whole design: transactions are timestamped by hashing them into an ongoing chain of computational proof-of-work, and the honest history is simply the chain that has the most computation behind it. As long as honest participants collectively control more computing power than any attacker, the honest history wins.

Key idea: replace trust in an institution with verification by anyone. Everything in the next eleven sections is machinery for making that swap actually work.
§ 02 · What a coin is

Transactions

“We define an electronic coin as a chain of digital signatures.”

This is the paper's most quietly radical sentence. A bitcoin is not a file, a token, or a number in an account. A coin is its own ownership history. To pay you, I take the last transaction that gave me the coin, add your public key to it, and sign the combination with my private key. Anyone can verify the signature chain from the beginning: every link proves the previous owner authorized the hand-off.

But signatures alone aren't enough. Nothing stops me from signing the same coin over to two different people — the double-spend problem. The old fix is a central mint that sees every transaction and keeps the one true ledger; but then the mint is a bank again and we've solved nothing.

The insight: the payee doesn't need to trust anyone — they need to know that at the time they were paid, no earlier spend of that coin existed. The only way to know that without a referee is for transactions to be publicly announced, and for all participants to agree on a single ordering of history. The rest of the paper builds that ordering machine.

§ 03 · Ordering events

Timestamp Server

“Each timestamp includes the previous timestamp in its hash, forming a chain, with each additional timestamp reinforcing the ones before it.”

How do you prove data existed at a certain time, and in a certain order, without a notary? Hash it and publish the hash widely — like printing it in a newspaper. A hash is a tiny fingerprint that could only have been produced from that exact data, so a published hash proves the data existed when it was published.

Now the trick that gives the blockchain its name: make each new timestamp include the previous timestamp's hash inside its own hash. Every new entry now mathematically vouches for the entire past. To alter one old entry you would have to recompute its hash — which changes the next hash, which changes the next, all the way to the present. History becomes a structure where the newest layer pins down all the older ones.

Key idea: chained hashes turn “a list of records” into “a list of records where editing the past is detectable by anyone in milliseconds.” Try it yourself in §4's tamper demo below.
§ 04 · Making history expensive

Proof-of-Work

“The proof-of-work involves scanning for a value that when hashed, such as with SHA-256, the hash begins with a number of zero bits.”

A hash chain makes tampering detectable — but anyone could still cheaply rewrite the whole chain and present it as a rival history. So the paper makes each link expensive to forge. To add a block you must find a nonce (a throwaway number) such that the block's hash starts with a required number of zeros. Hashes are unpredictable, so the only strategy is brute force: try nonce 0, 1, 2, … millions or quintillions of times. Each extra required zero bit doubles the average work.

The magic asymmetry: finding the nonce is brutally hard, but checking it takes one hash. A valid block carries built-in, self-verifying evidence that real computation was burned to create it. And because every block's hash includes the previous block's hash, rewriting an old block means redoing its proof-of-work and every block after it — while the honest network keeps extending the real chain.

This also solves the voting problem. Decisions can't be one-IP-one-vote (IPs are free to fake); they become one-CPU-one-vote, and the ballot box is the chain itself: the chain with the most accumulated work embodies the majority's verdict. A difficulty adjustment retargets the required zeros so blocks keep arriving at a steady pace no matter how much hardware joins.

// A block header commits to everything in the block.
// Change any field — even by one digit — and the hash is unrecognizable.
const header = {
  prevHash:   '00000000000000000002a7c4c1e48d76c5a37902165a2701...',
  merkleRoot: '871714dcbae6c8193a2bb9b2a69fe1c0440399f38d94b3a0...',
  time:  1231006505,        // Unix timestamp
  bits:  '1d00ffff',        // difficulty target, compact form
  nonce: 2083236893         // the number miners grind through
};

// Real Bitcoin double-SHA-256s the raw 80-byte header;
// hashing its text form shows the same idea.
const serialized = header.prevHash + header.merkleRoot +
                   header.time + header.bits + header.nonce;

print('header hash: ' + sha256(serialized));

print('now change the nonce by exactly 1…');
print('new hash:    ' + sha256(serialized.replace('2083236893', '2083236894')));
// → completely different output: this is the "avalanche effect",
//   and it's why mining can't be shortcut — only brute-forced.
code · a toy proof-of-work loop
// Proof-of-work in 10 lines: find a nonce that makes the hash
// start with `difficulty` zeros. Each zero multiplies the work ~16×.
const data = 'block #1: alice pays bob 5 BTC';
const difficulty = 3;
const target = '0'.repeat(difficulty);

let nonce = 0, hash = '';
const t0 = performance.now();
while (true) {
  hash = sha256(data + nonce);          // hash data+nonce together
  if (hash.startsWith(target)) break;  // lottery won — block is valid
  nonce++;                              // lost — try the next ticket
}
const ms = (performance.now() - t0).toFixed(0);

print('winning nonce: ' + nonce + '  (' + (nonce + 1) + ' attempts in ' + ms + ' ms)');
print('winning hash:  ' + hash);
// Verifying takes ONE hash — that asymmetry is the whole trick:
print('verify: sha256(data + nonce) starts with "' + target + '" ? ' +
      sha256(data + nonce).startsWith(target));
§ 05 · The protocol

Network

“Nodes always consider the longest chain to be the correct one and will keep working on extending it.”

The whole protocol is six steps, and none requires anyone to be identified or trusted:

  1. New transactions are broadcast to all nodes.
  2. Each node collects them into a candidate block.
  3. Each node grinds on the proof-of-work for its block.
  4. Whoever finds it first broadcasts the block.
  5. Nodes accept it only if every transaction inside is valid and unspent.
  6. Nodes show acceptance by building the next block on top of it.

Ties happen — two miners can find blocks at nearly the same moment, briefly splitting the network. No meeting is called; nodes just work on the branch they saw first, and the tie breaks itself when the next block lands on one side. That branch is now longer, so everyone hops to it. This is why the paper needs no perfect messaging: miss a block and the next one reveals the gap; the chain is self-healing gossip.

The consensus rule is a single sentence: the valid chain with the most accumulated proof-of-work is the truth. An attacker who wants a different truth can't argue, forge signatures, or stuff ballots — they can only out-compute the entire honest world, block after block, forever.

§ 06 · Why anyone bothers

Incentive

“He ought to find it more profitable to play by the rules … than to undermine the system and the validity of his own wealth.”

Mining burns real electricity — why would anyone volunteer? The paper's answer created the entire mining industry: the first transaction in every block mints new coins, payable to whoever mined it. This is elegant twice over. It rewards the security work, and it distributes new currency into circulation with no central issuer — the paper compares it to gold miners spending resources to add gold to circulation. The second income stream is transaction fees: the small difference between a transaction's inputs and outputs goes to the miner. Once the coin-minting schedule tapers off (it halves every four years), fees take over — the system is designed to eventually run inflation-free.

Then the game-theoretic keystone. Suppose someone actually assembles majority hash power. They could double-spend their own recent payments. But with that hardware they could instead mine honestly and collect more new coins than everyone else combined — while every attack undermines confidence in the very currency their fortune and hardware are denominated in. The system doesn't assume attackers are moral; it arranges for honesty to be the most profitable strategy available.

§ 07 · Pruning history

Reclaiming Disk Space

“Transactions are hashed in a Merkle Tree, with only the root included in the block's hash.”

If every node must keep every transaction forever, the ledger bloats. The fix: inside a block, transactions aren't hashed into the header one by one — they're paired up and hashed in layers, forming a Merkle tree. Only the tree's root (one 32-byte hash) goes into the block header. The root still commits to every transaction: change any leaf and the change cascades up and alters the root.

The payoff is surgical deletion. Once a coin's old transactions are long spent, a node can throw the branch away and keep only the connecting hashes — the header (and the chain built on it) stays intact and verifiable. Headers are ~80 bytes each, ~4 MB per year: the paper dryly notes storage “should not be a problem even if the block headers must be kept in memory.”

code · building a Merkle root
// A Merkle root: hash all transactions, then hash the hashes in pairs,
// layer by layer, until a single hash remains.
function merkleRoot(txs) {
  let level = txs.map(sha256);              // leaves = hashed transactions
  while (level.length > 1) {
    if (level.length % 2) level.push(level[level.length - 1]); // odd count → duplicate last
    const next = [];
    for (let i = 0; i < level.length; i += 2) {
      next.push(sha256(level[i] + level[i + 1])); // parent = hash(left ‖ right)
    }
    level = next;                           // climb one layer
  }
  return level[0];                          // the root — 32 bytes standing for everything
}

const txs = ['alice→bob:5', 'bob→carol:2', 'carol→dan:1', 'dan→erin:4'];
print('root:      ' + merkleRoot(txs));

txs[2] = 'carol→dan:100';                   // tamper with one transaction…
print('tampered:  ' + merkleRoot(txs));     // …and the root betrays it instantly
§ 08 · Light clients

Simplified Payment Verification

“It is possible to verify payments without running a full network node.”

Your phone shouldn't need the whole ledger to check that a payment cleared. SPV is the recipe: keep only the block headers of the longest chain (megabytes, not hundreds of gigabytes), then ask any node for the Merkle branch linking your transaction to a header — exactly the dashed hashes in the §7 demo above. If the branch hashes up to a root buried in the header chain, the network's miners have committed work on top of your transaction. Every block piled above it is another vault door.

The honest trade-off, stated plainly in the paper: an SPV client doesn't validate transactions itself — it trusts that the chain with the most work is honest. It can't be fooled while honest nodes dominate, but it's more exposed than a full node if an attacker ever overpowers the network. The suggested mitigations — accept alerts from full nodes, and businesses should run their own — are essentially how wallets still operate today.

§ 09 · Making change

Combining and Splitting Value

“To allow value to be split and combined, transactions contain multiple inputs and outputs.”

Coins from §2 are indivisible chains — you can't tear one in half to pay for coffee. So a real transaction is a small machine with multiple inputs and outputs: inputs gather value from previous transactions you own; outputs distribute it — typically one to the payee and one back to yourself as change, exactly like paying with a $20 for a $7 sandwich.

One transaction, paying 7 with a 5 + a 3
in: 5 (from tx A) in: 3 (from tx B) ⟶ out: 7 → merchant out: 1 → self (change)

Doesn't every payment then depend on a spidering web of prior payments? Yes — and the paper shrugs: it doesn't matter, because you never need to unpack the full history. The chain already proved every input valid the moment it was buried under proof-of-work. Validity is checked once, then compounded forever.

§ 10 · Pseudonymity

Privacy

“Privacy can still be maintained by … keeping public keys anonymous.”

Every transaction is broadcast to the world — the opposite of bank secrecy. The paper's answer is to move the privacy boundary. A bank hides transactions from the public but knows exactly who you are. Bitcoin publishes every transaction but identifies parties only as public keys — the world sees amounts moving between pseudonyms, “like tape-time in stock exchanges”: someone bought, the amount is public, the person is not.

Traditional model — privacy via gatekeeper
identities→ transactions→ trusted third party ‖ public sees nothing
Bitcoin's model — privacy via pseudonyms
identities ‖ public keys ⇄ transactions → everyone sees everything

Recommended hygiene: a fresh key pair for every transaction, so payments can't be linked to a common owner. The paper immediately flags its own weakness — multi-input transactions necessarily reveal that their inputs shared an owner, and one identity leak can unravel a whole cluster. That candid caveat spawned an entire field: blockchain forensics on one side, mixers and privacy coins on the other.

§ 11 · The math of finality

Calculations

“The probability drops exponentially as the number of blocks the attacker has to catch up with increases.”

The security claim gets quantified. Model an attacker secretly mining a rival chain to un-spend a payment: every block found is a coin-flip weighted by hash power — the attacker gains ground with probability q, falls behind with probability p = 1 − q. This is the classic Gambler's Ruin problem: a gambler with infinite credit but unfavorable odds. The chance of ever erasing a transaction buried z blocks deep is roughly (q/p)ᶻ — exponential decay. Each confirmation multiplies the attacker's odds by a number less than one.

The recipient's practical question — “how long do I wait before shipping the goods?” — is answered with the calculator below (a faithful port of the paper's own C code, Poisson-weighted over the attacker's possible head start). The famous result: against a 10% attacker, 6 confirmations push success odds below 0.1%. That is where the folk rule “wait for 6 confirmations” (~1 hour) comes from.

§ 12 · Nine pages later

Conclusion

“We have proposed a system for electronic transactions without relying on trust.”

The conclusion is one paragraph because the argument is complete. Retrace it: coins as signature chains give ownership (§2) but not ordering; a hash-chained timestamp server gives ordering (§3) but not cost; proof-of-work makes rewriting history expensive (§4); a gossip protocol with a longest-chain rule turns that cost into consensus (§5); block rewards make maintaining consensus profitable (§6); Merkle trees, SPV, and multi-input transactions make it practical (§7–9); pseudonyms make it tolerably private (§10); and the gambler's-ruin math makes the guarantee quantifiable (§11).

The closing note is easy to miss and very Satoshi: nodes “can leave and rejoin the network at will,” vote with their CPU, and need no coordination — “rules and incentives can be enforced with this consensus mechanism.” No launch plan, no company, no foundation. Two months later the genesis block was mined, and the paper's nine pages have run, unmodified in their essentials, ever since.

Supplementary — not in the 2008 paper

Beyond the Whitepaper

Concepts the last seventeen years bolted onto — or reacted against — Satoshi's design. None of these appear in the original nine pages; all of them assume you've understood them.

Keys in practiceWallets & Keys

A wallet stores no coins — only private keys; the coins live on the chain. Modern wallets derive endless key pairs from one master seed (those 12–24 backup words), automating §10's “new key per transaction” hygiene. Lose the seed and the coins are cryptographically orphaned forever: self-custody means the trusted third party you eliminated is now you.

Programmable transactionsSmart Contracts

Bitcoin transactions carry a tiny, deliberately limited script. Ethereum (2015) asked: what if blocks could carry arbitrary programs whose execution every node verifies? Money that enforces its own contract terms — escrow, auctions, lending — with no intermediary. The cost is a vastly larger attack surface: on a blockchain, a bug in a contract is not a patch away, it's permanent.

Consensus without miningProof of Stake

PoW's security bill is electricity. Proof of stake replaces “one CPU, one vote” with “one staked coin, one vote”: validators lock up currency as collateral and provably lose it if they cheat. Same goal as §4 — make rewriting history ruinously expensive — but the cost is capital at risk instead of energy burned. Ethereum switched in 2022; Bitcoin, deliberately, never has.

When rules changeForks

§5's tie-breaking forks resolve in minutes. But when the rules change, the split can be permanent: a soft fork tightens rules so old nodes still accept new blocks; a hard fork breaks compatibility, and if part of the community keeps mining the old rules, one currency becomes two (Bitcoin / Bitcoin Cash, 2017). Forks are the governance mechanism the paper never needed to specify.

~7 transactions/sec is not VisaScaling & Layer 2

Small blocks every ten minutes secure the chain but cap its throughput. Layer-2 designs like the Lightning Network move payments off-chain — two parties transact instantly and privately, settling to the chain only to open or close their channel. The base chain becomes what §7 hinted at: a settlement layer, not a payment rail.

What survived contact with realityThe Scorecard

Held up: the core consensus design — never broken in operation since 2009. Drifted: “one CPU one vote” became industrial ASIC farms and mining pools; most users hold coins via custodians — trusted third parties, re-invented. The paper solved trustless consensus; trustless usage turned out to be a human problem, not a cryptographic one.
