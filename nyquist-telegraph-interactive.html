§ 01 · The promise

Scope

“The required frequency band is directly proportional to the signaling speed.”

What this paper is: in 1928 telegraph engineering was a craft. Engineers knew, roughly, that faster signaling needed "better" lines, but each system — land line, submarine cable, carrier telegraph — had its own folklore. Nyquist opens by promising something different: a theory, with an actual limit in it. He lists eleven results up front, and three of them are the ones history kept:

First, the frequency band a telegraph needs grows in direct proportion to how fast you signal — double the speed, double the band. Second, when you decompose a telegraph signal into sinusoidal components, the frequency axis splits into bands each as wide as the signaling speed, and every band carries the same information — they are, in his word, mutually redundant. Third, and the punchline: the minimum band you must transmit equals the signaling speed, no matter how many distinct current levels you use.

He is careful to frame this as a theoretical ceiling: “a theoretical limit which cannot be attained in practice but which can be approached.” That framing — an ideal limit that real equipment approaches as engineering improves — is exactly the intellectual move Shannon would repeat for noise in 1948.

Key idea: this paper replaces "how good is my telegraph?" with "how close is my telegraph to the best possible telegraph?" — and then computes what best-possible means.
§ 02 · What a signal is

Preliminary Discussion

“The time is divided into approximately equal units which will be called time units… There is a finite number of conditions, and each time unit is characterized by a single one of these conditions.”

Before any mathematics, Nyquist does something quietly profound: he defines what makes telegraphy telegraphy. Two properties, he says, distinguish it from telephony: time comes in discrete equal units, and in each unit the sender chooses one of a finite set of conditions — key closed or open, positive or negative battery, or (in submarine cables) positive, zero, negative. This is the first clean definition of what we now call digital communication: discrete time, discrete alphabet. Speech has neither; Morse over a wire has both.

He then factors every signal into two independent pieces. The contribution of the h-th time unit to the wire's voltage is written as a product a_h · f(t):

  • the magnitude factor a_h — the number the sender chose (+1, 0, −1, …). This is the intelligence. It "is the same in all parts of the system and, in fact, in all systems transmitting the same signals."
  • the wave form f(t) — the physical pulse shape that carries it. This is determined by the equipment and the line, and "is not affected by the particular signal or form of intelligence being transmitted."

Content and carrier, cleanly separated — the same separation every modem datasheet still makes between "constellation" and "pulse shaping." He also defines speed of signaling s, in dots per second: the number of signal elements per second divided by two (a Morse "dot cycle" is one mark plus one space — two elements). And he restricts the whole theory to linear systems, pointedly excluding the ordinary clacking telegraph relay, which slams against mechanical stops.

Key idea: a signal = a list of numbers (a_h) × a reusable pulse (f(t)). Everything the paper proves works on the numbers, no matter what they encode.
§ 03 · The scaling law

Proportionality Between Speed of Signaling and Transmitted Frequency Band

“…for any given deformation of the received signal the transmitted frequency range must be increased in direct proportion to the signaling speed, and the effect of the system at any corresponding frequencies must be the same.”

The argument here is a beautiful two-paragraph scaling trick, no calculus required. Take any repeating telegraph signal and resolve it into its sinusoidal components (a Fourier series — components at the repetition frequency, twice it, three times it, …). Now imagine a second telegraph sending the same message but with every time unit half as long: everything happens twice as fast. Its Fourier decomposition is term-for-term identical — except every component sits at exactly twice the frequency.

So if the second system passes frequencies up to twice the first system's cutoff, and treats each doubled frequency the way the first treated the original, the received wave is an exact time-compressed replica: same shape, same deformation, half the time. Nothing about quality changed; only the band stretched in proportion to the speed.

Real systems in 1928 showed wildly different bandwidth-per-speed ratios, and Nyquist says why: they waste their bands to different degrees. Which raises the question the rest of the paper answers — “What is the limiting value for this factor under ideal conditions?”

Key idea: bandwidth and signaling speed are the same currency at different denominations. The interesting question is not the exchange rate's existence but its best possible value.
§ 04 · Redundant bands

Analysis of D-C. Wave

“Each such band contains all the information about the signal that the totality of bands from zero to infinity contains. One such band (or its equivalent) is necessary and sufficient to determine the original signal.”

Now the real analysis. Take N signal elements with magnitude factors a_1 … a_N, sent as rectangular pulses and repeated forever. The Fourier series of this wave has infinitely many components — but Nyquist shows (Appendix I) that every component factors into two parts:

  • a shape factor F(f) — depends only on the pulse shape (rectangular here), not the message;
  • a discrimination factor C(f) + iS(f) — depends only on the message (a_h's), not the pulse. It "determines that intelligence completely."

Then comes the counting argument that anticipates the sampling theorem. The message is N numbers. Each sinusoidal component gives you two measured numbers (amplitude and phase). So about N/2 components should suffice to solve for all N magnitude factors — and the discrimination factor obliges: it is periodic and mirror-symmetric, so its values in any one band of width equal to the signaling speed determine its values everywhere else. All other bands are copies. His Fig. 1 plots this for a 10-element test signal (1, 0, 1, 0, 0, 1, 0, 0, 0, 0) — the demo below recreates it live, for any signal you draw.

Look at what the chart shows regardless of the message you draw: band 2 is band 1 reflected, band 3 is band 1 negated, band 4 reflected-and-negated. Transmitting anything beyond the first band is sending the same letter twice. Hence the conclusion at the head of this section — and one more subtlety Nyquist flags: the constant (d-c.) component carries no intelligence either, so no line capacity need be spent on it.

Key idea: N numbers need only N/2 sinusoids ⇒ a band numerically equal to the signaling speed is necessary and sufficient. In modern language: 2W independent symbols per second through bandwidth W — the Nyquist rate.
§ 05 · The criterion

Distortionless Transmission

“For convenience the term nondistorting wave will be introduced and will be defined as a wave which produces perfect signals. A nondistorting wave may or may not be deformed.”

That second sentence is the paper's philosophical hinge. With a finite band, the received wave will be bent out of shape — the crisp rectangles arrive as rounded slopes. Nyquist's move is to stop caring. If the receiver only reads the wave at the mid-instant of each time unit, then only the mid-instant values matter; the wave is free to wobble however it likes in between. "Perfect" now means: value at each sampling instant proportional to the magnitude factor sent. Deformed, yet nondistorting.

Given that criterion, he computes (Appendix II-A) exactly which received-wave shape factors achieve it within the minimum band — and finds an entire family: the flat brick-wall spectrum, plus anything obtained by adding a component that is odd-symmetric about the signaling-speed frequency. Every member of the family produces a pulse that passes through zero at every other sampling instant, so neighboring symbols never speak over each other. This is the Nyquist criterion for zero intersymbol interference — the design rule inside every modem, DSL line, and Ethernet PHY built since.

He also works the criterion the other way: given the ideal received shape factor and the known rectangular sent wave, the required transfer admittance of the circuit is just their ratio, times a pure delay (a phase lag proportional to frequency shifts the wave in time without bending it). Build equalizers to give the line that characteristic and the ideal is realized. And because receivers differ, he shows the method generalizes: land-line telegraphs that read zero-crossing instants rather than mid-values, picture receivers that integrate exposure over the unit — each criterion yields its own family of ideal shape factors by the same computation.

Key idea: don't demand an undeformed wave — demand the right values at the right instants. That single relaxation makes minimum-bandwidth transmission possible and leaves infinite design freedom (the shape-factor family) to make it practical.
§ 06 · Carrier waves

Analysis of Carrier Wave

“On account of the mutual redundancy of the two sidebands, the total frequency range required in the symmetrical carrier case … for a given speed of signaling is just twice that required in the direct-current case.”

Carrier telegraphy — keying a tone instead of a battery, so many telegraph channels can share one wire at different pitches — was the hot technology of the 1920s. Nyquist redoes his analysis for it and finds an embarrassment: modulating a carrier splits the message into two sidebands, mirror images above and below the carrier frequency. Mirror images: the redundancy of §04, reborn. A symmetrical carrier channel spends twice the spectrum of a d-c. channel to say the same thing.

He then examines the two escapes, both of which became industry standards:

  • Phase discrimination: send two independent signals in the same band, one on a cosine carrier, one on a sine carrier. A product detector (his example: a dynamometer, one fixed and one moving coil) multiplied by the right reference responds to one signal and averages the other to exactly zero. Two messages, one band — full efficiency recovered. Today this is quadrature modulation, the "Q" in QAM.
  • Single sideband: transmit only one sideband. But he warns, from hard experience, that naively sliding the carrier toward the band edge slows the system down: the received wave decomposes into a fast in-phase part (the one you want), a sluggish in-phase part, and a quadrature part that builds up to roughly the derivative of the envelope. SSB works only with sharp filtering, phase correction at the band edges, and quadrature rejection — a checklist that telephone-multiplex engineers then spent a decade executing.
Key idea: modulation doesn't create information, it relocates it — and done carelessly, duplicates it. The two sidebands are the frequency-domain shadow of §04's redundant bands, and the cures (quadrature signaling, SSB) are now in every radio and modem standard.
§ 07 · Any pulse at all

Generalized Wave Form

“…the desired received current can be obtained in part by circuit design and in part by signal shaping at the transmitting end.”

So far the sent pulses were rectangles (d-c.) or keyed sinusoids (carrier). Here Nyquist generalizes to any repeated pulse shape — including pulses that deliberately spill into neighboring time units. The clean factorization survives: sinusoidal components still split into a shape factor (from the pulse) times the same discrimination factor (from the message). The whole §04–§05 theory carries over untouched.

The practical payoff is a new degree of freedom. The received spectrum is (sent shape factor) × (line's transfer admittance). Previously we fixed the sent pulse and bent the line with equalizer networks until the product was ideal. Now we can also bend the sent pulse — pre-distort the transmission so that the line's own imperfections complete it into the ideal. His Appendix VII even works out pre-shaping using stepped rectangular waves, the 1928 ancestor of a DAC-driven transmit filter. His engineering judgment on the trade-off: networks are simpler when moderate accuracy suffices; signal shaping is more accurate but more complicated. (Today's answer: do the shaping in silicon — every transmitter pre-equalizes.)

Key idea: the ideal received wave is a product requirement — sent spectrum × channel. You may satisfy it from either factor. Transmit-side pre-emphasis and receive-side equalization are the two halves of one equation.
§ 08 · Two languages

Dual Aspect of the Signaling Wave

“The important fact is that for telegraph purposes the two methods are substantially equivalent in result, though different in processes and labor involved.”

A telegraph wave can be described progressively — as a march of signal elements through time — or cyclically — as a stack of sinusoids in frequency. Nyquist lays the two vocabularies side by side, a Rosetta stone for the working engineer:

  • signal element ↔ sinusoidal component
  • magnitude factor a_h ↔ discrimination factor C, S (the intelligence)
  • wave form f(t) ↔ shape factor F(f) (the pulse)
  • indicial admittance A(t) (step response) ↔ transfer admittance Y(f) (the circuit)

— with the integral formulas to convert each pair (what we now call Fourier transform pairs). The two descriptions are logically equivalent, but not computationally equivalent: cascading circuits in the frequency domain is multiplication (addition, on a log scale), while the time domain demands differentiating, multiplying, and integrating measured curves — graphical operations that 1928 could not do precisely. Hence his verdict, and the whole paper's method: work in frequency. He closes the section noting the Fourier integral yields the same results as his Fourier series, "if care is taken" (Appendix IX spells out where care is needed).

Key idea: time-domain and frequency-domain views are the same physics in two notations — choose the one in which your problem is multiplication instead of calculus. Two decades later this dual bookkeeping is the standard furniture of signal processing.
§ 09 · The other limit

Interference

“…the number of distinct magnitude factors, hence, the amount of intelligence that can be transmitted over a circuit, are definitely limited by the interference.”

Everything so far assumed a clean line. Now the second wall. If interference is unpredictable — as likely positive as negative — a received level can only be read correctly if the interference stays below half the spacing between adjacent current values. Want more levels per element (more intelligence per second in the same band)? The levels crowd together, and the same interference starts flipping symbols. Noise, not bandwidth, caps the alphabet.

Put §04 and §09 together and you have, in 1928, both coordinates of channel capacity: bandwidth fixes how many symbols per second; interference fixes how many distinguishable values per symbol. What Nyquist did not have is a statistical theory of the noise itself — that gap is precisely what Shannon filled, turning "definitely limited" into C = W log₂(1+S/N).

Three more results round out the section. He coins the concept — and reports the term — intersymbol interference: the self-inflicted noise of a mistuned system, defined as the gap between the actual wave and the ideal one. He computes the penalty for running a system off its design speed: the interference is proportional to the slope of the transfer admittance, so sharply-filtered (minimum-band) systems are the most brittle — a real engineering trade that still bites. And he identifies the best transmit spectrum for overriding interference on a peak-power-limited line: the flat one, under which every extreme-level signal loads the line equally.

Key idea: bandwidth limits the symbol rate; interference limits the symbol alphabet. Multiply the two and you are one probability theory short of Shannon's channel capacity.
§ 10 · Synthesis

The Argument, Assembled

“The minimum band width required for unambiguous interpretation is substantially equal, numerically, to the speed of signaling and is substantially independent of the number of current values employed.”

Nine appendices of trigonometry back the main text — Appendix I derives the shape/discrimination factorization; II–III derive ideal shape factors for the mid-instant and zero-crossing criteria; IV–V do the carrier and single-sideband analysis; VI–VII generalize the pulse and design pre-shaping; VIII finds the power-optimal flat spectrum; IX reconciles Fourier series with the Fourier integral. But the spine of the argument is short, and worth retracing as one chain:

  • §02 defines the object: discrete time units, finite conditions — intelligence as a list of numbers riding a fixed pulse.
  • §03 shows by pure scaling that band and speed are proportional; only the constant is in question.
  • §04 computes the constant: the spectrum splits into bands of width s that all say the same thing, so one band — N/2 sinusoids for N symbols — is necessary and sufficient.
  • §05 makes the limit achievable: judge the wave only at sampling instants, and a whole family of band-limited pulses delivers every symbol untouched by its neighbors.
  • §06–§07 extend the same theory to carrier systems (exposing the sideband redundancy and its cures) and to arbitrary pulse shapes (splitting the work between transmitter and equalizer).
  • §08 certifies the frequency-domain method itself — the two aspects of the wave agree, one is just cheaper to compute in.
  • §09 closes with the other limit: interference bounds the usable alphabet, and with it the intelligence per symbol.

Read as a whole, the paper converts telegraphy from a craft into a pair of budgets — a spectrum budget and a noise budget — and proves exactly what each one buys. Every succeeding theory of communication, Shannon's above all, is an entry in those two ledgers.

01 · band redundancy of the discrimination factor — Fig. 1's signal 
// Nyquist's Fig. 1 test signal: 10 elements, magnitude factors:
const a = [1, 0, 1, 0, 0, 1, 0, 0, 0, 0];
const N = a.length;                     // band width s = N/2 = 5 harmonics

// Discrimination factor C(n) + iS(n) for harmonic n (Appendix I):
//   C(n) = Σ a_h cos(πn(2h+1)/N),  S(n) = Σ a_h sin(πn(2h+1)/N)
for (let n = 0; n <= 5; n++){
  const b1 = discrimination(a, n);      // band 1: harmonics 0–5
  const b2 = discrimination(a, N - n);  // band 2: mirror position
  const b3 = discrimination(a, N + n);  // band 3: shifted by N
  print('n=' + n + '  C=' + b1.C.toFixed(3).padStart(7) +
        ' | C(N−n)=' + b2.C.toFixed(3).padStart(7) +
        ' | C(N+n)=' + b3.C.toFixed(3).padStart(7));
}
print('');
print('C(N−n) = C(n) and C(N+n) = −C(n): every band repeats band 1.');
print('Transmit harmonics 0–5 (a band of width s) and you have it all.');
02 · minimum band: perfect at W = s, broken below it 
// Send 12 random two-condition symbols through an ideal band of width W,
// then read the wave at each mid-instant (receivedAt sums the shifted pulses).
const sent = Array.from({length: 12}, () => Math.random() < 0.5 ? -1 : 1);

function maxSampleError(wOverS){
  let worst = 0;
  for (let k = 0; k < sent.length; k++){
    const r = receivedAt(sent, k + 0.5, wOverS);  // mid-instant of unit k
    worst = Math.max(worst, Math.abs(r - sent[k]));
  }
  return worst;
}

print('symbols sent: ' + sent.join(' '));
for (const w of [1.5, 1.0, 0.9, 0.7, 0.5])
  print('W = ' + w.toFixed(1) + ' × s → worst mid-instant error = ' +
        maxSampleError(w).toFixed(6) + (maxSampleError(w) < 1e-9 ? '   ✔ perfect' : '   ✘ intersymbol interference'));
print('');
print('At and above the signaling speed: exact. Below it: neighbors bleed in.');
03 · deformed yet nondistorting: the ideal pulse family 
// Nyquist's §05: a wave may be deformed everywhere EXCEPT the sampling
// instants. Compare the brick-wall pulse (β=0) with a rolled-off one (β=0.6):
// between instants they differ — at the instants both are exact.
for (const beta of [0, 0.6]){
  print('β = ' + beta + ':');
  print('  p(0)    = ' + raisedCosine(0, beta).toFixed(6) + '   (the symbol itself)');
  for (let k = 1; k <= 3; k++)
    print('  p(' + k + 'Δ)   = ' + raisedCosine(k, beta).toFixed(6) + '   (neighbor instants → zero)');
  print('  p(0.5Δ) = ' + raisedCosine(0.5, beta).toFixed(6) + '   (between instants → anything goes)');
}
print('Both pulses are "nondistorting" — only one of them is undeformed. Neither');
print('matters: the receiver never looks between the mid-instants.');
Supplementary — not in the paper

Beyond the Paper

What happened to these ideas after February 1928 — the century of engineering that grew out of 27 pages of telegraph theory.

1928 → 1948From "intelligence" to "information"

The same year, Ralph Hartley (Nyquist's Bell Labs colleague) made the alphabet-counting idea quantitative: information = log of the number of distinguishable messages. Claude Shannon fused Nyquist's symbol-rate limit with Hartley's logarithm and a statistical model of noise into A Mathematical Theory of Communication (1948) — whose first page credits exactly this paper.

The sampling theoremNyquist–Shannon, and the Nyquist rate

Nyquist argued N symbols need N/2 sinusoids; Shannon proved the converse direction rigorously in 1949 (with independent versions by Whittaker 1915 and Kotelnikov 1933): a signal of bandwidth W is completely determined by 2W samples per second. "Sample above twice the highest frequency" — the Nyquist rate — now governs every ADC, CD player (44.1 kHz for ~20 kHz audio), and digital camera.

Zero-ISI pulses§05 became a filter catalog

The "family of ideal shape factors" with odd symmetry about f = s is known today as pulses satisfying the Nyquist ISI criterion; the raised-cosine member (demoed above) and its square-root twin are the default transmit/receive filter pair in telephone modems, DVB satellite TV, and most single-carrier radio standards.

Sidebands tamed§06 became QAM and SSB radio

Phase discrimination — two signals on quadrature carriers — is the I/Q modulation inside literally every modern transceiver; with multiple amplitude levels per phase it becomes QAM (Wi-Fi runs up to 4096-QAM). Single sideband, with the filtering and phase correction Nyquist prescribed, carried long-distance telephony for half a century and still rules amateur HF radio.

The other NyquistSame man, second landmark

Four years later Nyquist published Regeneration Theory (1932) — the Nyquist stability criterion of control engineering — and in 1928 he also derived Johnson–Nyquist thermal noise. One engineer, three fields, each with his name on its foundational limit. He retired from Bell Labs in 1954 holding 138 patents.

ScorecardWhat survived contact with reality

  • Band ∝ speed, minimum band = s — survived, verbatim; taught in every course. ✔
  • Mid-instant sampling & zero-ISI criterion — survived as the ISI criterion; receivers add matched filtering and adaptive equalization Nyquist could only pre-compute. ✔
  • Quadrature & SSB efficiency — survived; QAM is the workhorse. ✔
  • "Interference limits the levels" — right instinct, pre-statistical; superseded by Shannon capacity, which coding now approaches within fractions of a dB. ◐
  • Frequency-domain design over transients — survived so completely we forget it was ever a debate. ✔
