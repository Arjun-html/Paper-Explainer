§ 01 · What a message is

Introduction

“A general communications system … consists essentially of five elements: an information source, the transmitter, the channel, the receiver, and the destination.”

What it's actually setting up: before Shannon, engineers reasoned about telephones, telegraphs and radio as separate crafts. Shannon draws one diagram that fits them all — source → transmitter → channel → receiver → destination — and insists the only interesting question is how much can get through the channel, reliably, per second. Everything downstream in the paper is an answer to that single question.

Two definitions in this section quietly rebuild the field. First, following Nyquist and Hartley, information is measured logarithmically: a device with n positions stores log2 n units. Choose base 2 and the unit is the bit — the word makes one of its first appearances here. A bank of m flip-flops stores exactly m bits.

Second, if a channel can reliably distinguish M different signals of duration T, it carries log2 M bits in time T, so its capacity is the limiting rate C = lim (log₂ M)/T. The whole paper is the project of pinning down that M — first with no noise (§II–VI), then with noise (§VII onward), where “reliably distinguish” becomes the crux.

Key idea: strip away telephony, telegraphy and radio and every one is the same machine — a channel moving a bounded number of distinguishable signals per second. Count that number and you have measured communication itself.
§ 02 · Continuous from dots

The Sampling Theorem

“If a function f(t) contains no frequencies higher than W cps, it is completely determined by giving its ordinates at a series of points spaced 1/2W seconds apart.” — Theorem 1

This is the theorem that made digital audio, digital images and the entire sampled world possible. Its claim sounds impossible: a signal is a continuous curve with infinitely many points, yet if it is band-limited — no wiggles faster than W cycles per second — then a mere 2W samples per second capture it exactly. Nothing is lost; the continuous curve can be rebuilt perfectly from its dots.

The intuition Shannon gives: a signal with no frequency above W “cannot change to a substantially new value in a time less than one-half cycle of the highest frequency” — so checking it twice per fastest cycle misses nothing. The proof is a Fourier argument: the samples are the Fourier coefficients of the spectrum, and the spectrum determines the signal. Reconstruction uses one pulse per sample, the now-famous sinc function sin(πx)/(πx), which is 1 at its own sample and exactly 0 at every other.

Sample a signal, then rebuild it

A fixed band-limited waveform (three tones up to W). Set the bandwidth W and the sampling rate fs. The grey curve is the true signal, the dots are the samples, and the reconstruction is drawn on top by summing a sinc pulse at every dot. Drop fs below the Nyquist rate 2W and watch the rebuild peel away from the truth — that is aliasing.

Shannon also notes the count is symmetric in time and band: a signal limited to bandwidth W and duration T is fixed by 2TW numbers. Those numbers need not be evenly spaced samples — any 2TW independent measurements will do. That “2TW independent numbers” becomes the hinge of the entire geometric picture that follows.

code · reconstruct a signal from its samples
// Whittaker–Shannon interpolation (the paper's Eq. 7):
// f(t) = Σ  x[n] · sinc(fs·t − n),   sinc(x) = sin(πx)/(πx)
const sinc = x => x === 0 ? 1 : Math.sin(Math.PI*x)/(Math.PI*x);

// A band-limited test signal: one tone at 3 cps (so W = 3).
const f = t => Math.sin(2*Math.PI*3*t);

// Sample it ABOVE Nyquist (fs = 8 > 2W = 6) over a 2-second window.
const fs = 8, N = fs*2;
const x = Array.from({length:N}, (_,n) => f(n/fs));

const rebuild = t => x.reduce((s,xn,n) => s + xn*sinc(fs*t - n), 0);

// The samples pin the curve EXACTLY at every sample instant…
print('at a sample (t=5/fs):  true ' + f(5/fs).toFixed(4) + '   rebuilt ' + rebuild(5/fs).toFixed(4));
// …and reconstruct the values BETWEEN samples too:
print('between samples t=0.37: true ' + f(0.37).toFixed(4) + '   rebuilt ' + rebuild(0.37).toFixed(4));
print('the dots were enough to bring back the whole curve.');
§ 03 · A signal is a point

Geometrical Representation of the Signals

“The 2TW evenly spaced samples of a signal can be thought of as co-ordinates of a point in a space of 2TW dimensions.”

Here is the paper's master stroke. Three numbers are a point in 3-D space; likewise the 2TW numbers that specify a signal are the coordinates of a single point in a 2TW-dimensional space. A one-hour, 5-MHz television signal becomes one point in a space of 3.6 × 1010 dimensions. You cannot picture it — but you can reason about it with plain geometry, and that reasoning is exact.

The geometry pays off immediately because distance means something physical. The squared distance from the origin to a signal's point equals 2W times the signal's energy — i.e. d² = 2WTP, where P is average power. So all signals of power ≤ P live inside a sphere of radius √(2WTP). And noise, which nudges each coordinate a little, becomes a small blur around each point. This one dictionary — power↔radius, noise↔blur, filter↔projection — turns the capacity question into a packing question.

§ 04 · Messages worth sending

Geometrical Representation of the Messages

“All the points which are equivalent for the destination can be grouped together and treated as one point.”

Signals live in a space; so do messages, in their own space of 2T₁W₁ dimensions. But the message space is usually smaller than it looks, for two reasons Shannon draws out. First, the destination can't tell some messages apart — the ear is nearly deaf to phase, so many distinct waveforms sound identical. Group every indistinguishable message into one point (an “equivalence class”) and the effective dimension drops.

Second, the source may never produce most messages: speech must come from a human vocal tract; successive television frames are nearly identical. Improbable messages can be neglected. What's left is the effective dimensionality D — usually far below 2T₁W₁. This is the seed of data compression: you only need to transmit the dimensions that actually vary and that the receiver can actually perceive.

Key idea: the real size of a communication problem is not the raw waveform's dimensions but D, the dimensions the source can vary and the destination can notice. Compression is the art of finding D.
§ 05 · Modulation is a map

Geometrical Representation of the Transmitter and Receiver

“Every point in the message space must correspond to a point in the signal space, and no two messages can correspond to the same signal.”

Now the transmitter and receiver get geometric jobs. The transmitter takes one point in the message space and produces one point in the signal space — that is all modulation is, a mapping between two spaces. The one unbreakable rule: no two messages may map to the same signal, or the receiver could never tell which was sent. The receiver runs the reverse map; interestingly, it's allowed to send several signals back to one message (AM discards carrier phase, PCM tolerates pulse distortion) — that slack is exactly what buys noise immunity.

Shannon condenses the whole correspondence into one table. It is worth reading slowly: every communication concept on the left has become a piece of geometry on the right.

§ 06 · Trading band for noise

Mapping Considerations

“Any system which attempts to use the capacities of a wider band to the full extent possible will suffer from a threshold effect when there is noise.”

With modulation reframed as a map, Shannon reads off consequences from geometry alone. FM and bandwidth expansion: mapping the message line into a higher-dimensional signal space stretches it. The more the line wanders back and forth through the extra dimensions, the longer it gets — and a longer line means noise (a fixed small blur) is proportionally smaller, so the signal-to-noise ratio improves. That's why wideband FM sounds cleaner than AM: it spends bandwidth to buy quiet.

But there is no free lunch — hence the threshold effect. Fold a line densely into a plane and, once noise exceeds a critical size, the receiver can't tell which fold it's near, and the message jumps catastrophically. Going the other way, Cantor's mapping shows a square's points can be interleaved onto a line (digit by digit), so in principle bandwidth can be compressed arbitrarily when there is no noise — but the necessary discontinuity reintroduces a threshold the moment noise returns. This is where the naïve “Hartley's Law” both holds and fails, and it forces the question the rest of the paper answers: with noise fixed, what is the real limit?

§ 07 · The famous formula

The Capacity of a Channel in the Presence of White Thermal Noise

“By sufficiently complicated encoding systems it is possible to transmit binary digits at a rate C = W log₂ (P+N)/N with as small a frequency of errors as desired.” — Theorem 2

This is the result the whole paper was built to reach — the Shannon–Hartley theorem, arguably the single most consequential formula in communications. It says a channel of bandwidth W carrying signal power P against white noise power N has an exact, finite capacity, and — the shocking part — you can transmit right up to it with arbitrarily few errors. Below C: essentially perfect communication is possible. Above C: impossible, no matter how clever the code.

Theorem 2 — Channel Capacity C = W · log₂( (P + N) / N ) = W · log₂( 1 + P/N ) Bits per second, with error frequency as small as desired — and not one bit per second more.

The geometric proof, in one breath: in the 2TW-dimensional space, transmitted signals of power P land near the surface of a sphere of radius √(2TWP); noise blurs each into a little ball of radius √(2TWN). Received signals sit on a bigger sphere of radius √(2TW(P+N)). The number of noise-balls you can pack without overlap — the number of distinguishable messages — is at most the volume ratio of those spheres, and taking log₂ and dividing by T gives exactly W log₂(P+N)/N. Capacity is a sphere-packing count.

code · capacity, and the exchange relation
// Theorem 2, verbatim: capacity of a white-noise channel.
const capacity = (W, P, N) => W * Math.log2((P + N) / N);   // bits per second

// A voice-grade phone line: 3 kHz, signal-to-noise ratio ~1000 (30 dB).
const W = 3000, snr = 1000;
print('phone-line capacity: ' + Math.round(capacity(W, snr, 1)) + ' bits/s');

// Shannon's "exchange relation": T·W·log2(1+P/N) is the number of bits
// in time T. Halve the bandwidth and you must square the SNR to keep it.
print('at 3 kHz, SNR 1000 : ' + capacity(3000, 1000, 1).toFixed(0) + ' bits/s');
print('at 1.5kHz, SNR 1e6 : ' + capacity(1500, 1e6, 1).toFixed(0) + ' bits/s');
// Same throughput, different mix of band and power — you can trade them.

// The sanity check: at P = N (0 dB), every hertz carries exactly 1 bit/s.
print('C/W at 0 dB (P=N)  : ' + capacity(1, 1, 1).toFixed(3) + ' bit/Hz');

The crude pre-proof estimate (counting amplitude levels like PCM) gave a similar but weaker formula; Shannon shows the true optimum beats it by about 8 dB, because signals need only differ statistically over time, not at every instant. The price of reaching C is long codewords and long delays — the transmitter must wait for a whole block before it knows which signal to send. This block-coding insight is the ancestor of every error-correcting code in use today.

§ 08 · Diminishing returns

Discussion

“We will call a system that transmits without errors at the rate C an ideal system. Such a system cannot be achieved with any finite encoding process but can be approximated as closely as desired.”

Shannon lists what an ideal system looks like as you approach the limit, and the picture is almost paradoxical: the transmitted signal starts to look like random white noise (the best codewords are scattered randomly in the sphere), the required delays grow without bound, and the threshold effect sharpens — push noise past the design point and errors explode. Real systems (PCM, PPM) sit about 8 dB short of ideal, and whether closing that gap is worth the coding complexity is “a question of relative costs.” It took fifty years of coding theory to actually close it.

He also settles the bandwidth question. If noise power grows with bandwidth (N = N₀W), then widening the band helps a lot at first, then barely at all: capacity climbs toward a hard ceiling of 1.443 × (capacity at W₀), where W₀ is the band at which noise power equals signal power. More spectrum is not free capacity — it saturates.

§ 09 · Pour power where it's quiet

Arbitrary Gaussian Noise

“For frequencies where the noise power is low, the signal power should be high, and vice versa, as we would expect.”

Real channels aren't uniformly noisy — some frequencies are cleaner than others. If you have a fixed total power budget P to spread across the band, where should you put it? Shannon splits the band into thin slices, applies the white-noise result to each, and maximizes the total. The calculus-of-variations answer is beautiful and intuitive: choose the signal spectrum P(f) so that P(f) + N(f) is constant across every frequency you use.

Picture pouring water into an uneven basin whose floor is the noise spectrum: the water (signal power) settles to one flat level, deep where the noise floor is low, shallow where it is high, and — if you don't have enough water — the highest noise peaks stick out dry and get no power at all. This is literally called water-filling, and it is still how every modern modem, DSL line and Wi-Fi radio allocates power across sub-carriers.

code · the water-filling allocation
// Water-filling: choose P(f) ≥ 0 with Σ P(f) = P so that
// N(f) + P(f) = a constant "water level" wherever P(f) > 0.
function waterFill(noise, P) {
  // Find the level L such that the poured power equals P.
  const poured = L => noise.reduce((s,n) => s + Math.max(0, L - n), 0);
  let lo = Math.min(...noise), hi = lo + P + Math.max(...noise);
  for (let i = 0; i < 80; i++) {          // binary-search the waterline
    const mid = (lo + hi) / 2;
    poured(mid) < P ? lo = mid : hi = mid;
  }
  const L = (lo + hi) / 2;
  return { level: L, power: noise.map(n => Math.max(0, L - n)) };
}

const noise = [1, 2, 8, 3, 1, 5];      // noise floor per sub-band
const { level, power } = waterFill(noise, 12);
print('water level: ' + level.toFixed(2));
print('power/band : ' + power.map(p => p.toFixed(1)).join('  '));
print('the noisiest band (8) gets ' + (power[2] > 0 ? 'some' : 'ZERO') + ' power');
print('check: total poured = ' + power.reduce((a,b)=>a+b,0).toFixed(1) + '  (target 12)');
§ 10 · Noise's true size

The Channel Capacity with an Arbitrary Type of Noise

“For any noise, N₁ ≤ N. This is why white Gaussian noise is the worst among all possible noises.”

What if the noise isn't Gaussian — impulse noise, or noise through a nonlinear device? Shannon measures any noise not by its raw power but by its entropy power N₁: the power of the white Gaussian noise that is equally random. Two noises with the same entropy power blur each signal point into regions of equal volume, so they cost the same capacity — the entropy power, not the raw power, is what actually eats into the channel.

Theorem 3 — Capacity bounds for any noise W · log₂( (P + N₁) / N₁ ) ≤ C ≤ W · log₂( (P + N) / N ) where N is the noise power and N₁ ≤ N its entropy power; the bounds meet for white Gaussian noise.

Because N₁ ≤ N always, with equality only for white Gaussian noise, a profound corollary falls out: white Gaussian noise is the worst possible noise for a given power. Any structure in the noise — any deviation from pure randomness — is structure the clever coder can exploit to do better than the Theorem 2 rate. Nature's most featureless noise is also its most damaging, which is why the Gaussian channel is the standard pessimistic benchmark to this day.

§ 11 · Bits per symbol

Discrete Sources of Information

“It turns out that H is the number of bits produced by the source for each symbol of message.” — leading to Theorem 4

The paper pivots from the channel to the source. If a source emits symbols with a known probability structure — English text, where E is common and Q rare — how many bits does it truly generate per symbol? Shannon's answer is the entropy H = −Σ pᵢ log₂ pᵢ. It is a hard floor: Theorem 4 proves you can encode long messages into an average of about H bits per symbol, and no encoding can do better. Combine it with the channel: a source of entropy H can be sent over a channel of capacity C at up to C/H symbols per second.

The Appendix gives a concrete recipe (a Shannon–Fano code): sort symbols by probability, give each a codeword of length ⌈log₂(1/pᵢ)⌉ read off the binary expansion of the running cumulative probability. Probable symbols get short codes, rare ones long — and the average length lands between H and H+1. Build it yourself below.

code · entropy and a prefix-free code
// Entropy H = −Σ p·log2(p)  — bits the source makes per symbol.
const H = ps => -ps.reduce((s,p) => p > 0 ? s + p*Math.log2(p) : s, 0);

// Shannon's own example from §11: A, B, C with these probabilities.
const p = [0.6, 0.3, 0.1];
print('entropy H = ' + H(p).toFixed(3) + ' bits/symbol   (the paper says 1.294)');

// Shannon–Fano (Appendix): length ti = ceil(log2 1/pi),
// codeword = first ti bits of the cumulative probability.
function code(ps) {
  let cum = 0;
  return ps.map(pi => {
    const t = Math.max(1, Math.ceil(Math.log2(1/pi)));
    let x = cum, bits = '';
    for (let k = 0; k < t; k++) { x *= 2; bits += x >= 1 ? (x -= 1, '1') : '0'; }
    cum += pi;
    return { pi, t, bits };
  });
}
const cw = code(p);
cw.forEach((c,i) => print('  ' + 'ABC'[i] + ' (p=' + c.pi + '): ' + c.bits));
const L = cw.reduce((s,c) => s + c.pi*c.t, 0);
print('avg length L = ' + L.toFixed(3) + '  →  H ≤ L < H+1 holds');
§ 12 · Good enough, on purpose

Continuous Sources

“We cannot send continuous information exactly over a channel of finite capacity. Fortunately, we do not need to send continuous messages exactly.”

A continuous source — a live voice, a video — carries infinite information: pinning down one real number exactly needs infinitely many bits. So exact transmission over any finite channel is hopeless. Shannon's escape is to give up on “exact” deliberately: fix a fidelity criterion (say, tolerable RMS error √N₁), and suddenly the source has a finite rate again — the number of bits needed to reproduce it within that tolerance.

Geometrically, each message needs its own little sphere of “close enough” reproductions; count how many such spheres tile the message space and you get the rate. Theorem 5 bounds it, and in the clean case the transmittable condition is simply (W₁/2)·log₂(Q/N₁) ≤ W·log₂((P+N)/N) — source rate on the left, channel capacity on the right. This is the birth of rate–distortion theory: the formal reason a compressed photo or a streamed song can look and sound perfect while throwing most of the raw data away.

Key idea: "perfect" is the wrong target for continuous signals. Name how much error you'll accept and information theory hands you the exact minimum bit-rate to hit it — no more, no less.
§ ✦ · One argument, assembled

The Argument, Assembled

Retrace the chain and the paper reads as a single deduction. A band-limited signal is really just 2TW numbers (§II) — so it is a point in a 2TW-dimensional space (§III), where power becomes a radius and noise becomes a blur. Messages live in their own space, shrunk to their effective dimension D by what the source produces and the destination can perceive (§IV), and modulation is nothing but a mapping between the two spaces (§V). Reading that geometry off directly explains bandwidth-for-power trades and their unavoidable threshold cliffs (§VI).

Then the payoff: counting how many noise-blurred points pack into the signal sphere yields the exact capacity C = W log₂(1+P/N) (§VII), reachable but never exceedable, with sharply diminishing returns from bandwidth (§VIII). Coloured noise is handled by water-filling power into the quiet frequencies (§IX), and any noise at all is bounded through its entropy power, with white Gaussian proven worst (§X). Finally the source side: its irreducible rate is its entropy H (§XI), and continuous sources get a finite rate only once you fix a fidelity criterion (§XII). Source entropy on one side, channel capacity on the other, and a single inequality saying whether the message fits. In eleven pages, communication became a science with numbers.

Supplementary — not in the 1949 paper

Beyond the Whitepaper

What the last seventy-five years built on Shannon's foundations. None of this is in the original paper; all of it assumes it.

44,100 samples a secondDigital Audio & the CD

Human hearing tops out near 20 kHz, so §II demands at least 40,000 samples/second to capture it perfectly. The Compact Disc's 44.1 kHz rate is Theorem 1 turned into a consumer product — the little headroom above 2W leaves room for the anti-alias filter the §II demo makes you feel when fs drops too low.

Closing the 8 dB gapCapacity-Approaching Codes

Shannon proved C was reachable but gave no practical code; real systems sat ~8 dB short for decades. Turbo codes (1993), then LDPC and polar codes, finally crept to within a fraction of a dB of the limit. They power 5G, Wi-Fi 6 and deep-space links — the long block-codes of §VII, made real.

Water-filling everywhereDSL, Wi-Fi & MIMO

§IX's water-filling is not a curiosity — it is the daily arithmetic of every OFDM modem, splitting the band into hundreds of sub-carriers and pouring power into the clean ones. Multiple antennas (MIMO) extend it to spatial "sub-channels," multiplying capacity by using more of the sphere.

Good-enough by designRate–Distortion & JPEG

§XII's "name your tolerable error, get a finite bit-rate" grew into rate–distortion theory — the mathematical backbone of every lossy codec. JPEG, MP3, H.264 and their kin all trade a controlled amount of fidelity for a huge cut in bits, exactly as Shannon licensed here.

What survived contact with realityThe Scorecard

Held up completely: the sampling theorem and C = W log₂(1+P/N) are still exact and still binding — every link budget on Earth and in space respects them. Took longest to realize: the promised near-perfect coding, which needed 45 years of math to build. Nothing in the core has ever been overturned.
